# Pokémon 5e

Pokémon 5e plays differently than other D&D games you may have played before. No longer is your PC a powerhouse with a slew of dangerous spells and deadly weapons. You are now a Pokémon Trainer – a mortal in control of your Pokémon with an iron fist or a gentle heart. As such, though player characters have normal abilities and skills, your focus is not in hand-to-hand combat. Your ability to stay in the fight is dependent on the status of the Pokémon in your control. Although the threat of death is still very real, the battling and utility in the game is mostly transferred to your Pokémon.
___

This guide is an overview of special rules/changes in order to run an effective Pokémon role-playing game using existing 5e D&D rules. It is intended as a supplement to the core rulebooks of 5e Dungeons and Dragons.

# 1. Creating a Pokémon Trainer
___
You want to be the very best like no one ever was? Follow these guidelines to create your Pokémon Trainer! These rules are built to accommodate any 5e race, but it is assumed all players play the class of Pokémon Trainer.

## Class Features
 The Pokémon Trainer class has the following features:

#### Hit Points
___
- **Hit Dice:** 1d6 per level
- **Hit Points at 1st Level:** 6 + your Constitution modifier
- **Hit Points at Higher Levels:** 1d6 + your Constitution modifier.

#### Proficiencies
___
- **Armor:** None
- **Weapons:** None
- **Tools:** Pokéballs

___
- **Saving Throws:** Charisma
- **Skills:** Choose two from Animal Handling, Insight, Intimidation, Investigation, Medicine, Nature, Perception, Persuasion, Sleight of Hand, Stealth, Survival.

#### Equipment
You start with the following equipment, in addition to the equipment granted by your background:
- 5 Pokéballs
- 1 potion
- (a) a dungeoneer’s pack, (b) explorer’s pack, (c) scholar's pack, or (d) burglar's pack
- A Pokédex
- A starter Pokémon 
- ₽1000 + ₽10 x 4d4

### Trainer License and Bonds
Only licensed trainers are permitted to capture Pokémon. Their trainer license allows trainers to gain access to special services at Pokémon Centers and to purchase goods at the Poké Mart. Trainers can lose their license for attacking other trainers or their Pokémon, or failing to take care of their own Pokémon. Losing your license is also tied to how much your Pokémon trust you and your skills as a Pokémon Trainer. If your Pokémon distrust you enough they may leave your side. If such a thing happens too often, you may be required to turn in your license. How much your Pokémon trust you is shown by their Bond Level. You can read more about the Bond System in section 6.

### Starter Pokémon
A trainer must begin with any single unevolved Pokémon of CR 1/2 or lower. This Pokémon starts at Bond Level +1 (reference section 6 for information on the Bond System).

### Pokéslots
A Trainer can carry any number of Pokémon in their pack. However, a trainer can only use a number of Pokémon each day equal to their available Pokéslots. For each Pokeslot you have you must pick a single Pokémon to use that day. Once all of your chosen Pokémon for a day have fainted or been killed you can no longer send out any Pokémon during that day. You can change the list of prepared Pokémon when you finish a long rest. Trainers gain an additional Pokéslot at levels 5, 10, and 15.

### Pokédex
A hand-held computer with an advanced camera and image recognition software. A trainer can use an action to identify a Pokémon within 60 feet using the Pokédex's scanner.  Doing so registers the Pokémon on the Pokédex, and reveals the Pokémon's base CR, that particular Pokémon's level, and some brief facts about the species.

### Trainer Orders
At 6th level you have a basic understanding of how to work with your Pokémon as a team. As a bonus action you can give one Pokémon within 30 feet one of the following Trainer Orders. You must be able to see the Pokémon and it must be able to hear you. The Trainer Order lasts until that start of your next turn or until it ends prematurely. You can use Trainer Orders a number of times each day equal to your proficiency bonus. At the end of a long rest you regain all uses of Trainer Orders.
___
- **Attack Order:** Choose one creature you can see. You point out the target's weaknesses allowing your active Pokémon to have advantage on their next melee attack against that target.
- **Defend Order:** If your active Pokémon is vulnerable to any damage type, you can remove one of those vulnerabilities from your Pokémon until after it takes that type of damage.
- **Dodge Order:** The next time your Pokémon is forced to make a saving throw they may roll an additional 1d8 and add the number rolled to their result.

### Trainer's Resolve
By the time you reach 11th level, you have had your fair share of experience in difficult battles and have emerged victorious and strong. You are now immune to being frightened, and may choose a second saving throw to become proficient in.

### Pokémon Tracker
At level 13, you have already spent countless hours in the wild, searching for Pokémon high and low. Once per long rest you may search for Pokémon in the nearby area. You learn a list of wild Pokémon that can be found in the nearby area.

### Master Trainer
You have finally achieved the title of Master Trainer. Your Pokémon are at peak fighting performance. When you or your Pokémon fail a saving throw, you may choose to succeed instead. This feature can be used twice per long rest.

## Pokémon Control
The stronger the Pokémon, the more difficult they are to control. The strength of Pokémon you can control in the game depends on the level of your character as shown in the Max CR column of the Pokémon Trainer table. Pokémon with a higher CR than can be controlled can still be caught, but will not leave its Pokéball until the trainer is powerful enough to control them. The maximum Pokémon CR that you can control increases as you level up as shown in the table above.

Some DMs may wish to allow a trainer to control a Pokémon with CR greater than normally allowed. Perhaps that Pokémon evolved into a much more powerful Pokémon too soon or maybe it was a powerful ally that now wishes to travel with the party. Use your best judgement on what should be allowed and talk to your DM and players about bending this rule.

## Trainer Paths
There are many ways and reasons to train Pokémon, ranging from a desire for adventure to a wish to learn more about them. At the 2nd level choose one of the following paths:

### Ace Trainer
Your goal is to become one of the strongest trainers in the world, and so you excel in battle. All of your Pokémon gain a +1 bonus to their attack and damage rolls.
#### Battle Master 
When you reach level 5, you gain a number of battle dice (d6) equal to your Dexterity modifier. These die can be added to a single attack or damage roll. You can assign any amount of the dice to any of your Pokémon at the end of each of your long rests. A Pokémon with a battle dice can use one of those dice when they roll an attack roll or damage roll. Each day when you assign your battle dice for the day, any previous day's assignments are removed.
#### Rapid Switching 
When you reach level 9, your Pokémon can choose to take a single action or move on the turn they are switched. They still cannot use a bonus action or a reaction on that turn. Additionally, your battle dice become a d8.
#### Tactical Mastery 
Certain trainers choose to excel in one arena, utilizing it to its fullest potential. When you reach level 15, increase all of your Pokémon’s speed by 10. Then, choose an ability score. Each of your Pokémon increases that ability score by one. You must choose the same ability score to increase for all of your Pokémon. This increase will also apply to all Pokémon you catch in the future. Additionally, your battle dice become a d10.
___
### Hobbyist
You think being a trainer requires being very good at a little bit of everything. You can choose two of your skill proficiencies. Your proficiency bonus is doubled for any ability check you make that uses either of the chosen proficiencies. In addition, if your Pokémon are proficient in either of those skills, they too double their proficiency bonus to those ability checks.
#### Versatile 
At level 5, you gain a number of skill dice (d6) equal to your Intelligence modifier. These dice may be added to a single skill check or saving throw. You can assign any amount of these dice to yourself or any of your Pokémon at the end of each of your long rests.  Each day when you assign your skill dice for the day, any previous day's assignments are removed.
```
```
#### Tinkerer 
At level 9 you gain proficiency in tinker's tools. During each long rest, providing you have enough raw materials at your disposal, you can attempt a tinker's tools check to craft any one item under "Pokéballs", "X-Items", or "Other Held Items" in Section 10. Attempting the check costs you in resources equal to half the cost of the attempted item. The DC for these checks is equal to 10 + item's cost/₽250 (rounded down). Additionally, your skill dice become a d8.
#### Multitalented 
At level 15, whenever you capture or collect a new Pokémon, choose two of its ability scores to increase by 1. When you gain this ability, this bonus applies retroactively to Pokémon you have already caught. Additionally, your skill dice become a d10.
___
### Nurse
You have a nurturing touch and a skill for mentoring Pokémon to bring out the best in them. You gain proficiency in the Medicine skill. Every time you use an item or ability to heal a Pokémon, that Pokémon regains addtional hit points equal to your Wisdom modifier (minimum one).
#### Pokéchef
You excel at creating meals for your Pokémon, seemingly out of nothing. At level 5, you are frequently prepared with some sort of edible treat, granting 2d4+2 hit points when given. You can give the treat to a creature as a bonus action and other creatures can administer it as an action. You can use this feature a number of times equal to your proficiency modifier per long rest.
#### Warning Words
At 9th level, you can call out in battle, warning your Pokémon of incoming threats. When one of your Pokémon would suffer damage, you may use your reaction to reduce the incoming damage by 2d6 plus your Wisdom modifier. You may use this ability a number of times equal to your Wisdom modifier per day (minimum one).
#### Loyal to a Fault 
The care and attention you show to your Pokémon is returned in kind. When you reach level 15, your Pokémon have advantage on saving throws against negative status effects. Additionally, the amount you can reduce with your Warning Words ability increases to 3d6 plus your Wisdom modifier.

### Poké Breeder
Your dream is to care for Pokémon, giving them an environment where they can live and grow as well as guiding the trainers that use them. When you choose this path, you can add half your Charisma modifier (round down, minimum one) to any skill check that does not already benefit from your proficiency bonus. 
#### Frying Pan as a Drying Pan
Your knowledge of Pokémon anatomy makes you particularly skilled at healing. At level 5, you can cure one status effect affecting any Pokémon as an action. You can use this feature a number of times equal to your Charisma modifier per day.
#### Cheerleader 
You have the ability to inspire those around you. At level 9, once per short rest, you may use a bonus action to boost all allied Pokémon within 200 feet with inspiring words. Until your next turn, you may add your Charisma modifier to your choice of all allied Pokémon attack rolls, damage rolls, or AC.
#### Rally
When you reach level 15 in this path you can bolster the resolve of your Pokémon. As a bonus action, choose one Pokémon you can see within 120 feet to receive 1d8 + your Charisma modifier (minimum one) temporary hit points.

___
### Researcher
You wish to learn more about Pokémon and the secrets that they hold within. Due to your heightened understanding of your Pokémon, you may add your Intelligence modifier (minimum one) to any skill check your Pokémon makes. You may use this abiliy a number of times equal to your proficiency bonus per long rest.

#### Analyst
At level 5 your keen mind allows you to discern details about a Pokémon others might overlook. As an action or bonus action, you can make a Investigation check contested by a Pokémon’s Deception check. If you succeed, you learn that Pokémon’s abilities and the name of one random move it knows.
#### Evolutionary Expert 
At level 9, your understanding of the secrets behind Pokémon evolution allows you to influence the process. Your Pokémon gain 1 additional ability score point to put into any ability when they evolve. This bonus also retroactively applies to any Pokémon that evolved while under your care. Additionally, you may ignore the restriction of placing only 3 points in a single ability score when evolving.
#### Professor 
At level 15, you are an expert in your field. Your Analyst ability now also tells you the Pokémon's remaining HP and its AC. In addition, you learn that target's weak spots, granting your Pokémon +5 to all attack rolls against the Pokémon until the end of your next turn.

### Pokémon Collector
Your fascination with all the different types of Pokémon of the world drives your need to collect them all. Gain proficiency in the Animal Handling skill or another skill of your choice if you already have proficiency in Animal Handling. Your proficiency bonus for your Animal Handling skill is doubled.
#### Gotta Catch ‘Em All

#### Careful Catching 
At level 9, Your bonus from Gotta Catch 'Em All increases to +10 and any Pokémon you catch instantly are healed of their status ailments and brought to full health. Additionally you may choose at any time to attempt to catch a Pokémon as if it is at one third of its total health.
#### Disciplined Creatures 
At level 15, you have trained your Pokémon to hold onto their last bit of strength. When your Pokémon are brought to 0 HP, you can choose to bring it to 1 HP instead. You can only use this ability once per Pokémon per long rest.

### Pokémon Ranger
Your bond with the wild shows in your bond with your Pokémon. When you choose this Path, gain proficiency in the Nature and Survival skills. In addition, you may use the Help action as a bonus action.
#### Capture Styler
As a Pokémon Ranger you use a special tool to capture Pokémon called a Capture Styler. At level 5, if you have run all the way around a Pokémon, drawing a closed shape by the path you traveled, you gain a +10 to capturing that Pokémon.
#### Bestial Fury
At 9th level, you can unleash the primal animal within your Pokémon. As an action you can command your Pokémon to strike out in a flurry of attacks. That Pokémon can use a single target move with a Move Time of 1 action and a range of melee an amount of times equal to your Wisdom modifier while only using one PP. If you command a Pokémon to strike out in this way, that Pokémon cannot move or use actions until the end of your next turn. You can use this ability once per long rest.
#### Dominate Pokémon
At level 15, once per long rest as a bonus action, you may force an enemy Pokémon you can see with 60 feet to make a Wisdom saving throw against a DC of 8 + your proficiency modifier + your Wisdom modifier. If you or your allies are fighting the Pokémon, it has advantage on the saving throw. On a failure, that Pokémon follows your commands for one minute. Each time the Pokémon takes damage, it makes a new Wisdom saving throw against this effect. If the saving throw succeeds, this effect ends.

### Type Expert
You've dedicated your Pokémon journey to the study and training of only one type of Pokémon. When you choose this trainer path at level 2, choose a single type called your expert type. When one of your Pokémon hits a damaging move of your expert type, you can cause that move to deal maximum damage on that move's damage dice. You can use this feature a number of times equal to your Wisdom modifier per long rest (minimum one). 
#### Be The Very Best 
At level 5, you become keenly aware of how to channel your Pokémon's type in battle. Your Pokémon of your expert type that use a move of that type gain a bonus to the attack roll equal to your trainer Wisdom modifier (minimum one). Additionally, Pokémon of your expert type have the PP of moves of your expert type increase by a multiplier of 1.5 (rounded down).
#### I'm Gonna Have My Own Gym 
At level 9, your knowledge of your type extends beyond how your Pokémon fights. Whenever a Pokémon of your chosen type is your Active Pokémon, that Pokémon can gain advantage on a skill check. You can give your Pokémon this advantage a number of times equal to your Wisdom modifier per long rest (minimum one).
#### Type Resistance 
At level 15, you are so familiar with your type that what was once a glaring weakness is now easily overcome. Choose one type that your expert type is vulnerable to. Pokémon you own of your expert type can no longer be vulnerable to that type.

___
### Athlete
You're able to teach your Pokémon the art of physical exercise. Your Pokémon gain the ability to use the Dodge, Disengage, and Dash actions on their turns as bonus actions. You can have your Pokémon use these actions as a bonus a number of times equal to your Strength modifier (minimum one) per short rest.
#### Strength of Body
At level 5, your Pokémon become as tough and strong as you are. Once per round, your Pokémon can add your Strength modifier to any damage they deal with a move with a range of Melee.
#### Indomitable
A strong body is nothing without a strong mind. At level 9, you can use your reaction to cause one of your Pokémon to reroll a saving throw that they fail. If you do so, they must use the new roll. You can use this feature a number of times equal to your Strength modifier per long rest (minimum one).
#### Mastery of Self

# 2. Catching Pokémon
___
<div style='margin-top:20px'></div>


Catching Pokémon, much like in the original game, is crucial to your success as a Pokémon Trainer. There are several rules to follow when catching Pokémon:

- As previously stated, a trainer can carry any number of Pokémon. However, since a trainer can only use a number of Pokémon per day equal to their Pokéslots, they may choose to store Pokémon they capture in a second location.
- A Pokéball is destroyed on a failed catch attempt. 
- A caught Pokémon retains its level, all status effects, and current HP at the time of the catch.
- It is impossible for a Trainer to catch a Pokémon whose level is greater than their current level.
- A caught Pokémon starts with the minimum amount of experience for that Pokémon’s level.
- A fainted or dead Pokémon cannot be caught.
- If you catch a Pokémon instead of causing it to faint you do not gain experience from that Pokémon

There may be Pokémon in the wild that are friendly and happy to join you in your adventures. For those that need a little more coaxing, the mechanics of catching a Pokémon are much like casting a spell that everyone knows.
___

>#### Throw Pokéball
<br/>
- **Action Cost:** 1 action
- **Range:** 60 feet
- **Components:** 1 Pokéball of any type that is consumed upon capture attempt
>
>You hurl a Pokéball at a wild Pokémon in attempt to capture it. Make a Capture Roll by rolling a d20 and adding your choice of your Wisdom or Charisma modifier to the roll. If you are proficient in Pokéballs, you may add your proficiency bonus to the check.
You have advantage on the check if the Pokémon is poisoned, restrained, asleep, burned, confused, or frozen.
<br><br>
The DC to catch a Pokémon is equal to:
10 + Pokémon’s CR (rounded down) + Pokémon level + Fraction of HP remaining multiplied by 10 (rounded down)
<br><br>
*Note: Bonuses are added to your throw, depending on which type of Pokéball you use. See the item list for the different types of Pokéballs.*

# 3. Pokémon Care
___

## Active Pokémon
At each short or long rest, a trainer must choose an **Active Pokémon**. This Pokémon stays outside their Pokéball and roams around with the trainer to socialize and help with skill checks. In battle, they are the first one to fight until switched out.  A trainer can switch out their Active Pokémon at any time except immediately before entering a battle by taking the switch action (see section 4). If this Pokémon faints, the trainer must choose a new Active Pokémon at that time.

Pokémon, like trainers, are proficient in various skills. Outside of battle, a Pokémon may “help” their trainers, granting advantage on one of the trainer’s skill checks as long as the Pokémon is proficient in that skill. For example: Ash has chosen Pikachu as his active Pokémon. Pikachu is proficient in History and Performance. If Ash uses either of these skills, he is given advantage on the roll.

## Healing
The rules below slightly modify the D&D 5e rules on healing in order to make Pokémon 5e more like the Pokémon video games. However, if you are playing in a world where Pokécenters are not common, you may wish to modify these rules. There are three ways to heal your Pokémon.
- Bring them to a Pokécenter.
- Take a long or short rest.
- Give them potions or restorative food.

### Pokécenters
Pokécenters are small hospital-like facilities that can heal your Pokémon to full health and cure all their statuses in less than 30 minutes – for a small fee. Healing a Pokémon at a Pokécenter has a wide range of cost, usually around ₽100 to ₽1000. Pokécenters can be found across the world in abundance. Pokémon can recover their hit die only at Pokécenters.

### Rests
Long rests take at least eight hours and refresh all Pokémon health, statuses, and PP. Pokémon do not recover hit die during long rests.

Short rests take an hour and recover an amount of health equal to the number rolled on each of a Pokémon’s hit dice plus their Constitution modifier (as per normal D&D rules). PP and hit die are not recovered on short rests. Short rests do not heal status conditions.

### Potions/Food
These are items that can be given to a Pokémon as an action to instantly restore hit points, heal status effects, or temporarily increase abilities. These items cannot be given to Pokémon within Pokéballs (with some exceptions such as Revives). Potions and similar health restoring items can be used to bring a Pokémon from 0 HP to higher HP.

# 4. Battling
___
Being a master of Pokémon battles is core to being a Pokémon Trainer. Pokémon have their own set of moves, stats, and levels to gain. A Pokémon Trainer’s job on their turn is to assist their Pokémon in battle by interacting with the environment, providing potions or boosts, commanding their Pokémon to attack, or attempting to run away.

## Combat
Combat in Pokémon 5e is similar to D&D 5e, with a few exceptions:
- At the start of a battle, you roll one initiative for both you and your Pokémon using your trainer's initiative bonus.
- Only one of your Pokémon may be on the battlefield at any one time.
- You only have one action and one bonus action between you and your Pokémon.
- On your turn, both your trainer and your active Pokémon have a movement up to their speed.
- Both you and your Pokémon have one reaction you can use per round of battle.
- You MUST have a Pokémon in the battle at all times. If you have no remaining Pokémon (based on Pokéslots), you lose the battle and can rely only on yourself and your allies to get out of danger.

## Moves/Move Powers
Your Pokémon have several moves to choose from in battle, and can learn more as they advance levels or are given TMs and HMs. Moves in Pokémon 5e work similarly to spells in 5e D&D. Instead of a single character having a set amount of spell slots, each move has a set amount of **Power Points (PP)**. PP determines how many times the move can be used, one use per one PP. Refilling PP can be done by visiting a Pokécenter, long resting, or using an item such as Ether.

Each move has a **Move Power** – the focused ability of that move. Move Powers can be any of the six main ability scores , and determine which ability to use for attack, damage, and saving throw DC.
<div>
*Attack Roll Bonus = Move Power Mod + Proficiency Mod*<br>
*Damage Bonus = Move Power Mod*<br>
*Saving Throw DC = 8 + Proficiency Mod + Move Power Mod*
</div>

As shown in the calculations above, all Pokémon are proficient in all moves they know. As a single Pokémon can have many moves that use many different Move Powers, it can be difficult to keep track of the different attack bonuses, damage bonuses and saving throw. It might be helpful to write down those numbers next to each of your Pokémon's attacks.

Similar to spells in 5e D&D, if a Pokémon uses the Ready Action to ready a move, that Pokémon uses the PP immediately. This means that if the triggering event doesn't occur for that Pokémon's move before that Pokmeon's next turn, they use the move's PP without ever executing that move.

## Switching Pokémon
- A Trainer may take the "switch" action to recall one of their Pokémon within line of sight from up to 60 feet away and realease a new Pokémon within line of sight up to 60 feet away.
- Changing out a Pokémon before it has fainted takes up an entire action, but changing out a Pokémon when it faints can be done immediately and out of turn as a free action. 
- If a Pokémon is switched out as a free action as a result of that Pokémon fainting, the new Pokémon must be placed in the same space as the fainted Pokémon. 
- Alternatively, when a trainer's Pokémon faints they can recall the fainted Pokémon immediately and then wait until their turn to take an action to release a new Pokémon within 60 feet from their person.
- When a Pokémon is switched out it has to take a moment to take stablize itself after its recent reformation from pure energy. A Pokémon that has been switched cannot move, take any actions, take a reaction, or take a bonus action during the current turn.
- Switching out a Pokémon does not provoke an attack of oppurtunity on either the trainer or the Pokémon

## Attacks of Opportunity
If a Pokémon leaves the melee range of another creature without using the Disengage action, or when not returning to a Pokéball, the opponent may use a melee move that has a move time of 1 action immediately as a reaction targeting the fleeing Pokémon. The move costs the normal amount of PP.


## Trainers in Battle
In Pokémon battles,  a trainer's main job is to interact with the environment, issue commands, and keep their Pokémon in the fight by administering boosts or potions. There are strict laws governing the world of Pokémon and any trainer vs. trainer combat is strictly prohibited, the penalty being the loss of one's Trainer License. As such, trainers may not target other trainers in officially sanctioned Pokémon battles. However this is not the case in wild Pokémon battle or in battle against particularly nefarious opponents.

# 5. Types
___
Pokémon 5e replaces the standard 5e damage types (bludgeoning, slashing, force, thunder, etc.) with ones more typical to the Pokémon universe. These types are Fire, Water, Ice, Grass, Ground, Rock, Electric, Fighting, Ghost, Psychic, Poison, Bug, Flying, Dragon, Dark, Steel, Fairy and Normal. With these new types come new vulnerabilities and resistances as seen in the chart below. If a Pokémon resists a type, that type is "not very effective" (half damage). If a Pokmeon is vulnerable to a type, that type is "super effective" (double damage).

## Dual Typing
Many Pokémon have two distinct types rather than just one. To calculate the vulnerabilities and resistances of a multi-typed Pokémon, multiply the values in the column of their first type to the row of their second type. For example, a Normal/Psychic type Pokémon would be neutral to Fighting damage, resistant to Psychic, vulnerable against Bug, immune to Ghost, and vulnerable against Dark.

## Double Resistances or Vulnerabilities
It is quite possible that a Pokémon gains a resistance or vulnerability from both of its types. In this case the damage from that move only gets halved or doubled once, not twice. For example, Geodude is a Rock/Ground type and gains vulnerability to the Water type from both its Rock and Ground typing. If a Water-type move was to do a base of 6 damage to Geodude, he would take 12 damage, not 24. 

## Immunities
If a Pokémon is immune to a certain type,  it does not take damage from moves of that type. However, it still suffers other negative effects from those moves. For example, the Ghost-type Ghastly would not take damage from the Normal-type move Pound, but could still be put to sleep by the Normal-type move Sing.

# 6. Bond System
___
The bond between trainer and Pokémon is the heart and soul of any respectable trainer's abilities.

## The Basics
In addtional to the rest of their stats, each Pokémon has a Bond Level between -4 and 3. Usually the trainer of a certain Pokémon dictates how that Pokémon moves and acts, but with a sufficently low Bond Level, the Dungeon Master may intercede and control how a Pokémon acts. A Pokémon with a Bond Level below 0 will begin to act out, sometimes disobeying the trainer or even leaving the party altogether. On the other hand, a Pokémon with a high enough Bond Level will gain benefits.

When a Pokémon is captured from the wild, their Bond Level begins at 0. A Pokémon's Bond Level is specific to that Trainer/Pokémon pair. A Pokémon has a different Bond Level for each trainer it pairs with. If a Pokémon is ever returned to their original owner, they reset to the Bond Level they previously had with that trainer.

## Pokémon and Death
There are a few rules in Pokémon 5e that are different from regular 5e when dealing with the death of Pokémon. 
- When your Pokémon reaches 0 HP, it faints and you may immediately replace it with another Pokémon allocated to one of your Pokéslots as a free action.
- While the Pokémon is inside its Pokéball, it does not have to make death saving throws. 
- Additionally, Pokémon can avoid normal 5e instant death from taking negative damage equal to their maximum health if the Pokémon is recalled to a Pokéball upon taking that damage. 
- A Pokémon inside its Pokéball stabilizes at 0 HP after 10 minutes of uninterrupted time inside the ball. 
- If a Pokémon at 0 HP stays out in the field and it has not been stabilized or its trainer cannot recall it, it must begin making death saving throws as normal. 

Regardless, a Pokémon that faints may lose faith in their trainer and lose Bond Levels. 

## Manipulating Bond Levels
As you learn and grow with your Pokémon their Bond Level will fluctuate up and down. There are many ways to gain and lose Bond Levels, but below are some of the most common.

### Fainted Pokémon and Bond Levels
When your Pokémon faints, roll a d20. On a roll of 8 or lower your Pokémon loses one Bond Level. If enough of your Pokémon faint that you run out of Pokéslots, you "white out". When you white out you must roll a DC 12 Charisma saving throw for each of your fainted Pokémon. If you fail the saving throw, that Pokémon loses a Bond Level.

```
```
### Bonding
Bonding with your Pokémon is an important part of any trainer’s routine. Bonding with a Pokémon can take the form of practicing, eating together, playing games, or simply just enjoying one another’s company. When you've sufficiently Bonded with a Pokémon, you can make your choice of Animal Handling, Persuasion, or Nature skill check. The DC of this skill check is equal to 17 + the Pokémon's current Bond Level. On a success your Pokémon gains one Bond Level. 

Your DM determines the frequency of bond checks for your game. Bond checks should be done whenever a trainer and a Pokémon truly grow together as a team!

### Other Ways Of Changing Bonds
Sometimes a DM might decide that a Pokémon's Bond Level changes without a check. For example, if a Pokémon is deposited in a PC for longer than 4 months, they might lose a Bond Level. Or, if you and your Pokémon work together to win a particularly difficult or meaningful battle, they might gain a Bond Level. Anything from the tragic death of a loved one to a solid high five can change the pair's bond. It's up to the DM what ultimately changes a Bond Level, but the rules here hopefully give some guidelines.

## Evolution
Pokémon can evolve into a new form once they hit a certain level as determined in their stat block.  When a Pokémon evolves, the following occurs:
- It keeps its current ability scores, but gains 5 points to distribute towards any of those scores. No more than 3 of these points may be put into a single stat and no stat may be increased above 20.
- It gains an HP equal to its level.
- It acquires the hit dice of its evolved form for this level up and for furture rolls.
- It acquires the AC of its evolved form if that AC is greater than its current AC.
- The Pokémon might gain new proficiencies and new vulnerabilities/resistances as determined by its new form's stat block.
- It keeps the known moves it had at the time of evolution, but must learn all future moves from its new move list.
- If the Pokémon would receive other benefits at this level, it receives the benefits after it has evolved.
- Evolution can be delayed at the player’s discretion, but once the decision has been made, the Pokémon cannot evolve until gaining an additional level.

# 8. Other Changes
___
### New Status Effects
In addition to effects such as grappled, restrained, etc., Pokémon 5e adds or modifies several status effects. If an effect listed here has the same name as an effect from 5e D&D, use the effect listed here. If a Pokémon faints with one of these status conditions its condition, is healed when that Pokémon revives.

#### Poisoned:
A poisoned creature has disadvantage on all ability checks and saving throws, and takes 1d6 poison damage at the beginning of each of its turns until it faints or is cured of its poison. Poison and Steel-type Pokémon cannot be poisoned.

#### Frozen:
A frozen creature is incapacitated and restrained. It can attempt to break free of the ice with a DC 12 Strength save at the end of each of its turns. Ice-type Pokémon cannot be frozen.

#### Confused:
A confused creature is affected by confusion for three rounds. During this time, it loses its ability to take reactions and moves at half speed. It must roll a d20 at the beginning of each of its turns. On a result of 7 or lower, the creature hurts itself for 1d6 damage and ends its turn. At the end of each of its turns while confused, the creature can make a DC 16 Wisdom saving throw to end the confusion.

#### Burned:
A burnt creature deals half damage on all moves that use STR or DEX as their MOVE stat, and takes 1d6 fire damage at the beginning of each of its turns until it faints or is cured of its burns. Fire-type Pokémon cannot be burned.

#### Asleep:
A creature that is asleep is incapacitated, automatically fails all Strength and Dexterity saves, and falls prone.  A sleeping creature can make a DC 14 Constitution save at the beginning of each of its turns to attempt to wake up.

#### Flinched:
A flinched Pokémon has disadvantage on attack rolls. If a flinched Pokémon executes a move that requires a saving throw from the target, any targets have advantage on the roll. Being flinched lasts until the end of the creature's turn unless otherwise stated.

#### Paralyzed:
A paralyzed creature is incapacitated, cannot move or speak, fails any Dexterity or Strength saving throws, and attacks against it have advantage. A creature can make a DC 12 Constitution save at the end of each of its turns to escape paralysis. Electric-type Pokémon cannot be paralyzed.


### Challenge Rating (CR)
CR works slightly different in Pokémon 5e. Each Pokémon is given a “Base CR”. This is the challenge rating of the Pokémon at the lowest level it can be found. Think of CR as a number that signifies the strength of the species, not the specific Pokémon. For example, a level 1 Pidgey (Base CR 1/8) is not as strong  as a level 1 Bulbasaur (Base CR 1/2). However a level 5 Pidgey would be significantly strongther than a level 1 Pidgey even though they have the same CR. 
	
This new definition of CR gives a way to compare Pokémon species. This CR is only a rough estimate of the Pokémon's power. A particular Pokémon may situtationally become more powerful than its CR may signify. For guidance on giving out experience points for defeating Pokémon based on CR and level, see the *Running a Game* section and the tables in the Appendix.

### Mounts
Trainers can mount Pokémon that is at least one size larger than themselves. See page 198 of the 5th edition Player’s Handbook for rules on mounting.

### Pokémon Armor Class
A Pokémon's armor class is not based off its Dexterity score or any man-made armor it wears. Rather, each Pokémon has a base AC specific to its species, notated on each Pokémon's stat block. The AC of each Pokémon can be temporarily or permanently modified by feats, moves, level ups, and other effects.
	
### Trainer Death Saving Throws and Unconsciousness
Death is not as common in the Pokémon world as it is in 5e D&D. If you would like trainers to die less often, consider using this optional rule set. If a trainer is reduced to 0 HP, they make death saving throws as normal. However, they only need two successes to stabilize at 0 HP. Additionally, any medicine check to stabilize a trainer has advantage. A DC 15 medicine check can be performed on an 0 HP stable trainer to bring them to conciousness at 1 HP. An unconscious trainer's Pokémon will continue to follow the trainer's last command to its best ability unless such a command puts the Pokémon in mortal peril. In general a Pokémon with a higher Bond Level is more likely to act in line with its trainer's wishes while its trainer is unconscious.

# 9. Running a Game
___
### Building Encounters
There will be essentially two types of encounters in Pokémon 5e: Wild Pokémon encounters and Trainer Battles. Keep in mind that every Pokémon presented in this supplement's Pokédex is presented at its lowest possible level. To use Pokémon of higher levels as adversaries in battle, you will have to manually increase their stats. Use the table for Pokémon Advancement in Section 7 as a guide.

#### Wild Pokémon:
As a DM, you have a responsibility to continually introduce your players to new Pokémon of various levels throughout the campaign. Your trainers will want to go out into the wilderness to experience fighting and catching new Pokémon. It is important to remember that Pokémon of higher levels cannot be caught until the trainer has also reached that level, and higher leveled Pokémon help players progress through their levels without having to do much training. As such, capture-focused expeditions should contain several Pokémon of lower levels, while battle-focused  encounters should contain high level Pokémon to present a challenge to the player.

Because of a trainer's unique ability to capture the creatures they are fighting, it can be difficult to build wild Pokémon encounters. A wild Pokémon of medium levels that challenges your players can easily be caught and provides a significant and untimely boost to that player's power. This is not necessarily a bad thing, but rather a unique circumstance that a DM using this system should be aware of. Consider using a swarm of Weedle instead of a Beedrill, or perhaps giving wild Pokémon certain bonuses that disappear when they are captured by a trainer.

#### Trainer Battles
Often in a campaign, your players will come across other trainers that will want to engage in a Pokémon battle. Combat for these fights are similar to wild Pokémon encounters with a few differences:
- Another trainer’s Pokémon cannot be caught by any means.
- Trainer battles do not have to have the same number of trainers or Pokémon participating on both sides, but official trainer battles should have those numbers agreed upon before the battle begins.
- Pokémon belonging to a trainer act on that trainer's turn rather than on their own turn.
- In an official trainer battle, there must always be an Active Pokémon on the battlefield. If one side of the battle has no Active Pokémon for a whole round, that side loses.

To make trainer combat feel special, consider giving certain important or powerful NPC trainers unique abilities or more powerful Pokémon. Never forget that the bond between trainer and Pokémon is of upmost importance in Pokémon D&D. Trainers in battle should be willing and ready to act in battle as best they can to support their Pokémon.

### Starting Adventures Past Level 1
You may want to start a game with trainers higher than level 1. If this is the case, make sure you follow these additional rules:
- If your are using Option 1 for character advancement, the total levels of a trainer’s Pokémon cannot exceed the minimum level as shown in the experience chart.
- None of the trainer’s Pokémon can have a CR above what they can control based on their level.
- One of the trainer’s Pokémon should be of a CR ½ or lower as their starter.

### Experience Points
Experience for Pokémon encounters should be given out at the discretion of the DM based on participation in the fight by each PC, or distributed equally among all players. The amount of experience given out is based on both the opposing Pokémon's CR and its level. See the Appendix for an example table on rewarding experience for defeating Pokémon.

# 11.  Feats

Any feature not related to combat from the 5e PHB can be selected for trainers. The following feats can be selected by your Pokémon instead of taking the Ability Score Increase given during the leveling process.

#### Extra Move:
Your Pokémon can know one more move (five total moves instead of four). You may later take this feat again to gain the ablity to know one more move (to a maximum of six moves total).

#### Extra AC:
Your Pokémon’s AC increases by 2. This bonus is included through a Pokémon’s evolutions and is separate from any AC Ups obtained through leveling. This feat can only be taken once.

#### Tireless:
Your Pokémon endures hours of rigorous training which keep it in battle longer than the average Pokémon. Gain +1 PP for every move.

#### Terrain Adept
Your Pokémon is especially skilled when fighting on a specific terrain. Choose one of the following terrains when selecting this feature. Your Pokémon gains +4 to attack rolls when in this terrain.
Terrains: Coastal, Swamp, Forest, Arctic, Desert, Grassland, Hill, Mountain, Underwater.

#### Loyal
When your Pokémon would lose a Bond Level, roll a d20. On a result of 12 or higher, they do not lose that Bond Level.

#### Ambidextrous
Your Pokémon can hold an addtional held item.

#### Prodigy
If this Pokémon is not currently counting towards your level advancement as per option 1 in the trainer advancement section, all experience points gained by this Pokémon are doubled.

#### Scholar
When this Pokémon is taught a TM, it does not destroy the TM.

#### Natural Healer
As an action, your Pokémon can restore hit points to a creature equal to 1d6 + your Pokémon's level. The creature can't regain hit points from this feat again until it finishes a short or long rest.

#### Natural Mount
While this Pokémon is mounted, if the creature mounting it is attacked, it can force the attack to target itself instead. Addtionally, if this Pokémon or its mounter are forced to make a Dexterity saving throw that would result in half damage on success, it can deploy additional defenses. This Pokémon can choose either itself or its mounter to take no damage if they succeed on the saving throw, and only half damage if they fail.

#### Proficient

#### Specialized
Choose one move this Pokémon knows. That move gains +2 maximum PP.
